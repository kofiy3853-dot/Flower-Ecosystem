Composting Guide

Turn kitchen scraps and garden waste into nutrient-rich compost — the foundation of healthy soil

What Is Composting?

Composting is the natural process of recycling organic matter — like leaves, food scraps, and yard waste — into a valuable fertilizer that can enrich soil and plants. It's nature's way of breaking down dead material into nutrients that feed new growth.

By composting at home, you reduce landfill waste, cut greenhouse gas emissions, and create a free, nutrient-dense soil amendment that improves soil structure, water retention, and microbial activity.

What to Compost

Compost These

  • Fruit & vegetable scraps
  • Coffee grounds & filters
  • Tea bags (non-synthetic)
  • Eggshells (crushed)
  • Yard trimmings & leaves
  • Grass clippings
  • Shredded newspaper/cardboard
  • Wood chips & sawdust
  • Houseplant trimmings
  • Hay & straw

Avoid These

  • Meat & fish
  • Dairy products
  • Oils & grease
  • Diseased plants
  • Pet waste
  • Treated/painted wood
  • Synthetic materials
  • Coal/charcoal ash
  • Invasive weed seeds
  • Glossy/colored paper

Getting Started in 5 Steps

  1. 1

    Choose Your Method

    Pick a composting method based on your space, how much waste you produce, and how hands-on you want to be. A simple cold compost pile is easiest for beginners.

  2. 2

    Pick a Location

    Choose a dry, shady spot near a water source. Direct soil contact helps beneficial organisms migrate into your pile. Avoid placing directly against structures.

  3. 3

    Build Your Pile

    Layer brown materials (dry leaves, cardboard) with green materials (food scraps, grass). Aim for a 2:1 ratio of browns to greens by volume.

  4. 4

    Keep It Moist & Aerated

    Your pile should feel like a wrung-out sponge. Turn it every 1-2 weeks to add oxygen and speed decomposition. Cover during heavy rain.

  5. 5

    Harvest & Use

    Compost is ready when it's dark, crumbly, and earthy-smelling. Screen out large pieces and return them to the pile. Mix into garden beds or use as top dressing.

Benefits of Composting

  • Reduces landfill waste — Up to 30% of household waste is compostable
  • Enriches soil — Adds nutrients, improves structure, and boosts microbial life
  • Retains moisture — Compost-amended soil holds water better, reducing irrigation needs
  • Suppresses diseases — Healthy compost outcompetes harmful pathogens
  • Reduces need for chemicals — Less reliance on synthetic fertilizers and pesticides
  • Sequesters carbon — Composting diverts organic waste from landfills where it produces methane
  • Saves money — Free soil amendment instead of buying bags of compost or fertilizer

Quick Tips

  • Chop materials small (1-2") for faster breakdown
  • Keep a kitchen caddy for daily scraps
  • Add a handful of finished compost as starter
  • Cover with a tarp to manage moisture
  • Balance nitrogen (greens) and carbon (browns)
